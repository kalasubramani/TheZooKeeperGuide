Zoo Keeper's Guide

Note: This website provides an insight into the personal characteristics of each of our zoo inhabitants. This page does not provide details about the diet plans or cage/habitat management for any of the zoo inhabitants. You can reach out to your Supervisor to obtain those details.

As a zoo keeper intern, it is essential for you to identify each inhabitant and understand their needs here and personal behaviors.

Notes to Zoo Keeper Interns

** Click on the images of the zoo inhabitants to further reading about their natural habitat,distribution across the globe and general knowledge facts.

Bears

  1. Ollie

    Ollie, exudes a calm and gentle presence, captivating visitors with her glossy black fur and soulful brown eyes. Resting in the shade of the trees in her spacious enclosure, Ollie often appears contemplative, observing her surroundings with a quiet curiosity. Visitors can observe her in moments of repose, lounging comfortably or indulging in a leisurely stroll around her habitat. Her interactions are characterized by a sense of calm assurance, creating an environment where visitors feel a deep connection with this magnificent creature. She loves fish and honey.

  2. Mona

    Mona, the charismatic bear at the zoo who steals the hearts of visitors with her incredibly playful personality. She often engages in entertaining antics, like rolling around in a specially designed pool, splashing water joyfully in all directions. Her large, powerful paws make quick work of manipulating toys and objects scattered around her habitat. Her agility and dexterity are impressive as she balances on rocks or logs, showcasing her playful nature. Mona loves to interact with her audience, and she's particularly fond of children.

Giraffes

  1. Frankie

    Frankie the giraffe is quite the character at the zoo, known for his insatiable appetite and enthusiastic gulping of food. Standing tall with his distinctive long neck and striking coat of patches, Frankie's eyes light up with anticipation at the sight of any edible treats. Whether it's crisp leaves, crunchy vegetables, or specially prepared giraffe pellets, Frankie approaches every meal with gusto. His long tongue, which can extend up to 45 centimeters, skillfully wraps around branches or reaches into feeding buckets, ensuring that no tasty morsel escapes his attention. He can guzzle up to 45kg of leaves and twigs a day! Frankie is social and easygoing.

  2. Coconut

    Coconut, the rare white male giraffe, is a captivating and elusive presence within the zoo. Standing tall with his majestic white coat, Coconut is a striking figure that commands attention and awe. His lack of traditional spots sets him apart from other giraffes, making him a rare gem in the animal kingdom.

    Despite his majestic appearance, Coconut is known for his shy and reserved character. He often prefers the outskirts of his enclosure, seeking solace in the shadows of tall trees or behind natural features. His demeanor is characterized by a gentle and introverted nature, and he tends to be cautious in his interactions with both other giraffes and zoo visitors. Poachers kill rare white giraffes. He is very precious for our zoo.

Lions

  1. Mella

    Mella, the lioness, is a magnificent and proud resident of the zoo, and she recently assumed the role of a protective and caring mother. As the "big mommy," Mella has given birth to a lone, precious cub, and her demeanor has shifted to one of heightened protectiveness and defensiveness.

    Visitors can observe Mella pacing back and forth near the den, a clear indication of her watchful and maternal instincts as she keeps a vigilant eye on her cub. Due to Mella's protective nature, access to her and the cub is strictly limited. Only senior zookeepers, with their wealth of experience and understanding of Mella's behavior, are entrusted with handling her. This precaution is in place to maintain a stress-free environment for the lioness and her cub, prioritizing their health and comfort.

    The sight of her with her cub serves as a heartwarming reminder of the circle of life within the zoo, and visitors can appreciate the natural instincts and protective instincts of this majestic "big mommy" lioness.

  2. Karl

    Karl, the rare white lion cub, is a living treasure at the zoo, hailing from the esteemed Timbavati Private Nature Reserve in South Africa. White lions like Karl hold a special place in the hearts and traditions of local tribes in Timbavati, where they are regarded with high esteem and often considered sacred.

    As the youngest member of the zoo's pride, Karl is a symbol of rarity and beauty. His pure white coat, a result of a recessive genetic trait, makes him stand out in contrast to the more common golden hues of other lions. At just three months old, Karl embodies the innocence and curiosity of youth.

Monkeys

  1. Cookie

    Cookie, the tamarin monkey, is a pint-sized bundle of curiosity and sociability that steals the hearts of both staff and visitors alike. With his lively personality and mischievous charm, Cookie brings an extra dose of joy to the zoo.
    This little guy is a natural observer, and it's not uncommon to find him perched in his exhibit, carefully watching the zoo staff as they go about their duties. His inquisitive nature makes him particularly interested in the activities of his human caretakers, and he often seems to be studying them with a discerning gaze.
    One of Cookie's distinctive behaviors is his love for scent marking. Anything new that enters his environment becomes an instant candidate for a touch of Cookie's personal ownership. From exhibit features to the keepers themselves, Cookie enthusiastically marks everything with tiny hugs, showcasing his endearing way of establishing connections.
    When it comes to snack time, Cookie's playful demeanor shines even brighter. He eagerly makes his way onto the keeper's shoulder, showcasing his trust and affection.

  2. Earl

    Earl, the proboscis monkey, is a fascinating member of his primate family, known for his striking resemblance to humans and his entertaining antics. Proboscis monkeys are renowned for their distinctive long noses, potbellies, and expressive faces, giving them an uncanny human-like appearance. They are too human!!
    One of Earl's favorite pastimes is displaying his agility by frequently leaping from tree limbs and making daring splashes into the water below, much to the delight of onlookers. His comical belly flops and acrobatic maneuvers add a touch of humor to his otherwise regal demeanor, making him a standout character in the zoo.
    However, despite his playful nature, there is a crucial aspect to Earl's care – he should not be fed bananas under any circumstances. Bananas are known to be fatal to proboscis monkeys.

  3. Banana Pudding

    Banana Pudding, the squirrel monkey, is a petite and charming character at the zoo, known for his boundless inquisitiveness and impressive intelligence. With his expressive eyes and lively demeanor, Banana Pudding has become the sweetheart of both visitors and zookeepers alike.
    One of his standout features is his insatiable curiosity. Beyond his curiosity, Banana Pudding has gained a reputation for his exceptional sociability.
    Adding to his remarkable abilities, Banana Pudding has become quite the puzzle-solving sensation. His intelligence shines through as he easily tackles a Rubik's Cube, manipulating the colorful squares with precision. This unique skill not only captivates audiences but also highlights the cognitive abilities of squirrel monkeys.

Alligators

  1. Wren

    Wren, the freshwater crocodile, is an intriguing and formidable resident of the zoo. With his sleek, scaled exterior and watchful eyes, Wren exudes an air of ancient mystique. However, unlike some of his more sociable zoo counterparts, Wren prefers to maintain a respectful distance from humans.
    Known for his wariness of human presence, Wren is not one to seek out interaction. His preference for solitude is a natural aspect of his disposition, aligning with the instincts of a creature adapted to life in the water.
    Wren's self-protective instincts are evident, and it's crucial for zoo staff and visitors alike to exercise caution. Despite his reserved nature, Wren is capable of delivering a nasty bite if he perceives a threat within his vicinity. As a result, only senior zookeepers, equipped with the experience and understanding of Wren's behavior, are allowed to enter his designated area for maintenance and care.
    The strict access policy ensures both the safety of the zookeepers and the well-being of Wren.

  2. Aspen

    Aspen, the dwarf caiman at the zoo, is a captivating and distinctive reptile that adds an element of intrigue to the exhibit.
    Despite his entertaining nature, Aspen comes with a cautious note for those looking after him. Dwarf caimans, like Aspen, have a natural instinct to bite when they feel threatened or in danger. This characteristic adds a layer of complexity to interacting with Aspen, emphasizing the importance of understanding his behavior before attempting any close contact.
    Zookeepers, including those who aspire to become reptile experts like the intern, are advised to exercise patience and restraint when working with Aspen. It's crucial to respect his space and refrain from getting too close until a thorough understanding of his behaviors, needs, and potential stressors is acquired. This cautious approach is essential for both the well-being of Aspen and the safety of the zookeepers.

  3. Mika

    Mika, the gharial, is a majestic and impressive resident at our zoo, earning the endearing title of the "big mommy." Stretching close to 15 feet in length, Mika commands attention with her powerful yet elegant presence.Despite her imposing size, Mika is known for her shy nature. She tends to shy away and hide from human presence, preferring the seclusion of her habitat.
    Mika's affinity for fish is a well-known aspect of her diet. Her eyes light up with excitement during feeding times, showcasing the natural behaviors of a gharial.
    It's crucial to note that Mika's species is classified as Critically Endangered, highlighting the vulnerability of gharials in their natural habitats. The zoo takes on the responsibility of contributing to conservation efforts through the care and preservation of individuals like Mika. Regular monitoring is essential to ensure her well-being, and any interactions with her must be approached with the utmost care and respect for her natural instincts.
